Executive Summary

This study reports the findings from two surveys of Kansas hospitals, municipal and county governments, and school districts concerning their preparedness for the Year 2000 (Y2K) problem. The first survey was conducted in the first quarter of 1998, while the second was conducted in the last quarter of 1998.

The overall purpose of these surveys is to present information that is useful to decision makers in addressing Y2K preparedness among these local organizations in Kansas. In providing this information, this report assesses the degree of preparedness for the Year 2000 problem in these four categories of organizations, determines the extent of change in the preparations of these organizations when comparing the findings of the first survey with the findings of the second survey, and analyzes what, if any differences there are by population characteristics.

 

A comparison of the findings of the first and second surveys shows that hospitals, counties, cities, and school districts have improved their Y2K preparedness. Variables that were strong to moderate predictors of Y2K preparedness in the first survey (population, supervising board awareness, and type of organization) are generally not as strong of predictors of Y2K preparedness in the second survey. These findings suggest that Y2K preparedness is beginning to permeate into all different types of local organizations. Hospitals continue to lead other organizations in their aggregate level of Y2K preparedness, however, counties, cities, and schools have improved their levels of Y2K preparedness and in some areas, surpass the levels of Y2K preparedness of hospitals. In addition, organizations in smaller communities still tend to lag behind those in larger communities, but the extent to which they are "behind" has declined.

 

While there is considerable evidence of improvement, the second survey still shows that there are many areas of concern. At this late date, there is still a substantial percentage of local organizations that are in the beginning stages (awareness or assessing) of Y2K preparedness especially in the areas of automated computerized systems (embedded systems) and external business support systems. Administrative systems show the highest levels of Y2K preparedness for all four types of organizations.

 

Perhaps the most pressing problem that needs immediate attention is in the area of contractual liabilities related to outside vendors being contractually bound to insure that their systems and services will continue to operate properly after the year 2000. Less than half of all organizations have made these types of contracts. When broken down by organization type, about 33% of counties and 35% of hospitals have made some determination of contractual liabilities. Only about 25% of schools and municipalities have made some determination. Because of the legal implications that are expected to result from Y2K problems, programs need to be initiated as soon as possible to address this area of concern.

 

Another cause for concern is in the area of contingency planning. Only 47% of organizations have an emergency plan in the event of Y2K related failures. Perhaps even more disturbing is that of those organizations that are uncertain of the possible impact of Y2K on critical services, 61% do not have an emergency plan.

 

Finally, among all four types of organizations, there is an increasing percentage that are uncertain of the impact that Y2K will have on their ability to deliver mission critical services. This is disturbing because, it suggests that the more activities these organizations engage in to address Y2K problems, the less certain they become that their efforts will be enough.

 

All this strongly suggests that efforts on the part of all concerned need to intensify between now and the end of the year to ensure that these organizations are prepared for the year 2000.

 

Section 1: Introduction

 

The "Year 2000 Problem" has been recognized in scholarly and professional writing for over a decade (de Jager, 1993). Many undesirable consequences can flow from the "Y2K" problem, as it will be called here. Best said Y2K is a very simple problem with staggering implications.

 

When programmers first designed information technology still in use today, two digits (mm/dd/yy) were used instead of four (mm/dd/yyyy) to conserve memory which was initially very expensive. Many of the two digit systems and software calculate the length of time between dates by subtracting two-digit years from each other. Software performing arithmetic operations or sorting on date fields may fail or yield incorrect results when working with the year beyond 1999. If an application uses a two-digit date format, the computer may read "00" as "1900" instead of "2000." This same error occurs with all types of systems including invoicing, credit card transactions, inventory systems, databases and payrolls. In addition, data files can be lost or corrupted.

Embedded Systems

 

The problem extends beyond software and data integrity. At the heart of the Y2K problem is the Achilles heel, the "embedded chip." Almost every electronic product used today relies on some type of embedded device. The first item that comes to mind is usually a home or work based PC. But as we go through a normal working day, our use of vehicle anti-lock brakes, fax machines, laser printers, telephones, photocopiers, and the automated teller machine also involves interaction with embedded systems.

 

The embedded chips are the micro-chips that drive much of the technology used by government, healthcare and business as well. Chips in water and sewage systems, pacemaker monitors, security systems, air traffic control units, credit cards, automated factories and billing systems could all be negatively impacted by the Year 2000.

 

According to Costis Toregas, President of Public Technology, Inc., experts predict that only about two percent of the 25 billion chips in use today will fail. Since there is no way to tell which two percent will fail, it is necessary to test systems with embedded chips and prepare contingency plans for any mission critical systems with embedded devices.

 

Other Dates

 

Attention to the Y2K problem naturally focuses on events occurring near the beginning of the year 2000; however, problems can be encountered both before and after the beginning of the century. Key dates other than 1/01/00 include:

  • 8/21/99 GPS rollover problems
  • 9/09/99 End of file problem
  • 10/01/99 Fiscal year end problem
  • 1/4/00 First working day of the year problem
  • 2/29/00 Leap year problem (phase I)
  • 12/31/00 Leap year problem (phase II)

Prior Findings

 

In this study, we are concerned with assessing the state of readiness for the Y2K problem for Kansas county and municipal governments, hospitals and school systems. An identical study was conducted in the first quarter of 1998. At that time, the responses showed a wide range of results with some organizations being well prepared and other organizations that had not started assessments. A summary of organizations by type was as follows:

 

  • County governments reported the greatest level of Y2K awareness by their boards, and were relatively confident about their preparations for delivery of services. Over 80 percent of county governments had made an assessment of the Y2K. These organizations were the best prepared in assessment of relations with businesses that provide goods and services.
  • Municipal governments reported lower levels of confidence about preparedness in most areas as compared to the county governments. Nearly 25 percent of municipalities believed the Y2K problem will negatively impact administrative services. As of March 1998, approximately 37 percent of municipalities had developed plans to maintain critical services.
  • Hospitals (with county governments) reported the highest level of preapration in may aspects of delivery of services. Over 49 percent of hospitals were unsure if Y2K would negatively impact their critical systems and 11 percent believed that Y2K would negatively impact critical service delivery. Forty percent of the hospitals did have contingency plans in place as of March 1998, to ensure that critical services would be maintained.
  • School Districts were the least likely to have done the vital assessment study or have boards which were aware of the Y2K problem. Only three percent of schools believed that Y2K would impact critical services.

Municipalities and County Governments

Cities and counties are faced with not only business risks but public safety issues as well. Emergency response systems, police departments, transportation control systems for streets, highways, mass transit and utilities are all vulnerable. According to one city government official, their internal awareness program has already begun to pay off. "We have been discussing Y2K for many months, but it was only after a half day training session with all our staff from the road crew to the Mayor, that we began to see our city employees work together on Y2K solutions and contingency plans."

The National League of Cities (NLC), the National Association of Counties (NACo), and the International City/County Management Association (ICMA) have joined with Public Technology, Inc., a non-profit technology research organization to launch the Y2K & YOU campaign. The campaign is designed to help local governments ensure that their communities are "Y2K compliant" and that public health and public safety are not compromised. The campaign also includes a "Y2K tool kit"and video which can be ordered for $27 by calling 1-800-784-8976. Association members may receive free copies of the Y2K tool kit by e-mailing their name, title, jurisdiction name, and address to y2k@pvcla.com.

Lubbock, Texas has received national press attention for their Y2K awareness and contingency preparedness efforts. Bob Cass, Lubbock city official, testified before the Senate Special Committee on the Year 2000. "I believe the primary reason the Senate Staff asked us to be present was the fact that we ran a simulation that anticipated various Y2K failures on December 31, 1999," said Cass. The complete findings and examples of the Y2K drill can be found at:

http://y2k.ci.lubbock.tx.us/excersise.htm

The city government in Lubbock sees the Year 2000 as an opportunity to increase citizens awareness about emergency preparedness, and to make sure that their municipal government is prepared to continue delivering the services their community expects. According to the Mayor, "the City of Lubbock is taking measures to prepare for the worst case scenario, but we are not forecasting this outlook on Y2K. We expect very few disruptions caused by January 1, 2000."

Hospitals

Hospitals face a unique challenge with regard to the year 2000. All businesses face a financial risk, but hospitals must also address patient safety concerns. Dr. Kenneth Kizer, VA Undersecretary testified to Congress last summer that Y2K could cause radiation machines to emit the wrong doses of radiation or an ICU unit could inaccurately read a patients heart rhythms unless the software was reprogrammed for the year 2000. In addition, hospitals must assess Y2K risks with outside parties including pharmacies, pathology laboratories, outside medical suppliers and emergency crews.

Of those hospitals that have taken a very active approach concerning Y2K, it appears clear that the Board of Directors had a high degree of involvement and financial support for the project. "We put people in charge of certain Y2K projects and set deadlines," said one Y2K coordinator. "There are 101 things that people would rather be doing than Y2K but, you have to make it the number one priority if you expect to succeed" he said.

Another important factor in addressing Y2K seems to include an employee education campaign and a good public relations program. "People are calling our hospital wanting answers" said one western Kansas Hospital Administrator, "the staff knows that all inquiries are ultimately referred to certain legal or public relations experts on staff." Internal Y2K messages have been disbursed through bulletin boards, presentations, global e-mails or internal publications to ensure that the hospital staff understands the seriousness of the problem and the importance of consistency in spreading the Y2K message. An intensive care unit supervisor believes educating core emergency staff is critical, "it's important that our nurses and doctors understand the potential problems with Y2K so that should a situation arise due to Y2K, we have backup procedures in place."

Schools

Unlike hospitals, schools will not be expected to be open for business on Saturday, January 1, 2000. Nonetheless, the schools will need to ensure that student records, financial systems, heating and cooling systems and food service delivery systems are compliant. The Department of Education is taking steps to ensure that all elementary and secondary schools are prepared for the Y2K. The Department of Education recommends that schools visit the Department's website:

http://www.ed.gov/y2k

On the site, educators will find "Y2K Best Practices" for elementary and secondary school systems. The Department has also compiled a publication entitled "Year 2000 Compliance Guide of Elementary and Secondary Schools, " which can be ordered by calling 1-877-4ED-PUBS.

 

Section 2: Study Methods

This study reports the findings from two surveys of Kansas hospitals, municipal and county governments, and school districts concerning their preparedness for the Year 2000 (Y2K) problem. The first survey was conducted in the first quarter of 1998, while the second was conducted in the last quarter of 1998. The findings of the first survey were reported to the State of Kansas in the Docking Institute study "Year-2000 Readiness of Kansas Public Institutions: Hospitals, Local Governments, and Schools."

This report assesses the degree of preparedness for the Year 2000 problem in these four categories of organizations, determines the extent of change in the preparations of these organizations when comparing the findings of the first survey with the findings of the second survey, and analyzes what, if any differences there are by population characteristics. For purposes of comparison, both questionnaires (Appendix A) contained the same six major sections:

1. General questions

2. Information technology for critical services

3. Systems that may process dates

4. External business support

5. Administration

6. Contact data

 

The format for the questions in sections two through five were based on a continuum with response categories ranging from "implemented" (i.e., the problem is regarded as solved) to "no activity" (i.e. the organization has not begun to deal with the problem). The response categories were defined as:

  1. Implemented: implemented converted or replaced platforms, applications, databases, utilities, and interfaces. Ready to implement data exchange contingency plans, if necessary.
  2. Validating: Testing, verifying, and validating converted or replaced platforms, applications, databases, and utilities. Testing the performance, functionality, and integration of converted or replaced platforms, applications, databases, and utilities. Testing the performance, functionality, and integration of converted or replaced platforms, applications, databases, utilities, and interfaces in an operational environment.
  • Renovating: Converting, replacing, or eliminating selected platforms, applications, databases, and utilities. Modifying interfacing.
  • Assessing: Assessing the Year 2000 impact on the organization. Identifying critical services and processes, developing inventories and analyzing systems supporting critical services, and prioritizing conversion or replacement. Developing contingency plans to handle data exchange issues, lack of data, and bad data. Identifying and securing the necessary resources.
  • Awareness: Defining the Year 2000 problem and gaining executive level support and sponsorship. Establishing a Year 2000 program team and developing an overall strategy. Ensuring that everyone in the organization is fully aware of the issue.
  • No Activity: Have not begun to assess if the organization has a Year 2000 problem.
  • Not Applicable: This question is not applicable to the activities of the organization.

This framework has been used widely in the computing industry as a standard for expressing the stage of an organization's encounter with the Y2K preparations (see, e.g., Hall, 1998). For the first section, question response categories were answered "yes," "no," or "don't know." This format was also used in one question of the section on external business support.

The first questionnaire was administered during the first quarter of 1998. A total of 848 questionnaires were sent. After two mailings, 633 usable surveys were returned, for a response rate of 75%. The second survey was administered in the last quarter of 1998. A total of 848 questionnaires were sent. After three mailings, 700 usable surveys were returned for a response rate of 83%. The response rates for each type of organization are shown in Table 1. The relatively high response rates (over 50% for each category and 75% and 83% overall) suggest the surveys are broadly representative of counties, cities, school districts, and hospitals. Further analysis of the demographic characteristics of these organizations suggests no apparent response bias by population and region. Inasmuch as the attention of this study is on Kansas institutions, inference to counties, cities, school districts, and hospitals outside of Kansas is not appropriate.

Section 3: Survey Results

A comparison of the findings of the first and second surveys shows that hospitals, counties, cities, and school districts have improved their Y2K preparedness. Variables that were strong to moderate predictors of Y2K preparedness in the first survey (population, supervising board awareness, and type of organization) are generally not as strong of predictors of Y2K preparedness in the second survey. These findings suggest that Y2K preparedness is beginning to permeate into all different types of local organizations. While this is an improvement, the second survey still shows that there are many areas of concern. At this late date, there is still a substantial percentage of local organizations that are in the beginning stages (awareness or assessing) of Y2K preparations. This is a cause for concern because most consultants capable of providing Y2K services to these local organizations are booked well into the year 2000. Whether these organizations have the local capacity to address their Y2K preparedness is a question that these surveys do not address, however, the Chief Information Technology Officer of the State of Kansas is undertaking a statewide effort to provide local organizations with additional information to facilitate Y2K preparations.

What Kinds of Organizations Are in Trouble?

An urgent question for policymakers is, of course, the identification of organizations that show a low level of Y2K preparedness. In the first report, we noted that organizations with a low level of Y2K preparedness could be characterized as:

  • Having significantly lower governing board awareness.
  • Being more likely to be a rural community.
  • Being more likely to be a school district.

 

Figure 1 shows the overall response pattern to the question "Have you made an assessment of the ability of your computer systems to correctly process the Year 2000?" Whereas only 65% of local organizations had done so in the first survey, in the second survey over 88% report that they have done an assessment of their computer systems.

 

 

 

Figure 2 shows that governing board awareness is still important for predicting if the organization has done a Y2K assessment. In the first survey, 78% of organizations whose supervising boards were aware had done a Y2K assessment. In the second survey, this percentage increases to 92%. For those local organizations whose governing boards are not aware, the picture has improved since the first quarter of 1998. In the first survey, only 46% of those organizations whose supervising boards were not aware had done an assessment, while in the second survey, this percentage increases to 63%. The bad news is that 37% of these organizations have not done Y2K assessment. These findings continue to show the importance of supervisory board awareness for Y2K preparedness in local organizations.

 

 

Figure 3 shows the relationship between assessment and the population of the community. For each population category, there has been an increase in the percentage of organizations that have done an assessment when comparing the findings from the first survey with the second survey. While organizations in the smallest communities are still the least likely to have done a Y2K assessment, the percentage of organizations in the smallest communities that have done an assessment increases from 45% to 79%. This is a significant increase in less than one year.

 

 

 

 

Figure 4 shows the relationship between assessment and the type of organization. The first survey showed that both school districts and municipalities lagged behind the efforts of hospitals and counties. The responses in the second survey show that these two governmental units have caught up to hospitals and counties (over 80%). Significantly, almost all hospitals have done an assessment.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Responses to Questions on Other General Topics

 

We begin analyzing the results of the survey by examining the difference between the first and second survey for the following five general topic questions.

  • "Adverse Impact on Services": Will the Year 2000 computer processing problem adversely effect the delivery of critical services like water, electricity, sewer, law enforcement, fire protection, emergency medical services, medical equipment operation, health care, etc.
  • "Adverse Impact on Administration": Do you expect that the Year 2000 problem will negatively impact administrative services such as billing systems?
  • "Emergency Plan": Do you have a plan that will allow you to maintain critical services in the event that any system fails?
  • "Fee Increase": Do you expect that programs to mitigate the Year 2000 problem will cause your organization to raise taxes, fees, or rates?
  • "Board Awareness": Is your supervising board aware of your organization's Year 2000 situation?

Table 2 shows the patterns of responses on each of these five questions for both surveys. Except for the last question on board awareness, the response patterns from both surveys are similar. A somewhat disturbing development in the second survey is that there is an increase in the percentage of local organizations that simply do not know if Y2K will have an adverse impact on the delivery of critical services. This percentage increases from 36% in the first survey to 42% in the second survey.

Given this level of insecurity, it is also discomforting that the percentage of organizations with an emergency plan only increases from 37% on the first survey to 47% on the second survey. Still less than half of all organizations lack contingency plans to address emergencies due to Y2K.

 

On the positive side, both surveys indicate that only 5% of organizations expect a fee increase because of Y2K problems.(1) In addition, in the second survey over 79% of local organizations did not think that Y2K will adversely affect administration. For the delivery of critical services, we explore the question as to whether certain types of organizations are more likely to expect Y2K to hamper their ability to deliver critical service? Figure 5 shows that consistent with the first survey, hospitals and schools are least certain of the impact of Y2K on their critical services. Over 55% of hospitals and 50% of schools are uncertain if Y2K will have an adverse impact on the delivery of critical services. This is in comparison to less than 35% for counties and cities. Municipalities continue to be the most certain that they will not experience service problems from Y2K. Only 33% of cities are uncertain that Y2K will have an adverse impact on critical services. Significantly, Figure 5 shows that consistent with patterns noted in Table 2, this growing uncertainty is consistent across all organizational types.



For emergency planning, we explore the question of whether certain types of organizations are more likely to have an emergency plan in the event of Y2K problems. The answer is yes. First, about 68% of hospitals have an emergency plan, compared to 37% of counties, 40% of schools and 47% of cities. Second, those organizations in the most populous areas are more likely to have an emergency plan. About 55% of organizations with populations of 7,204 and above have an emergency plan, compared to 46% for populations between 945 and 7,203, and only 38% for those under 945. Third and most disturbing, is that of those organizations that are uncertain of the possible impact of Y2K on critical services, 61% do not have an emergency plan. (see Figure 6). Even those organizations that answered no to the question of whether Y2K would adversely impact their critical services, are more likely to have an emergency plan (about 51%).

  • "HEVAC": Building Controls (heating, air conditioner, humidity, etc.)
  • "Elevators"

  • "Radio": Radio Communications (dispatch, logs, controls, etc.)

  • "Plant Operations": Automated Controls for Waste Water, Electricity, Medical Equipment, etc. (plant operations)

 

The responses for these questions are shown in Table 4 (next page). The most significant aspect of Table 4 is for each system, the percentage of organizations responding not applicable declines between the first and second surveys. For example, the percentage reporting not applicable for telephone systems declined for 55% in the first survey to 35% in the second. With a decline in the percentage of organizations stating not applicable, it makes sense that there was an increase in the percentage reporting some level of Y2K preparation activity in the second survey. For example, with HEVAC systems, the percentage assessing went from 12% in the first survey to 24% in the second. Likewise, the percentage reporting that they have implemented their HEVAC Y2K preparations increases from 6% to 15%. This consistent pattern across each computerized system suggests that education is extremely important to understanding the types of systems that may be effected by Y2K problems. In the first survey, organizations may not have been aware that systems like these could be effected by Y2K problems.

 

For purposes of this set of analyses, we have combined the responses implemented, validating, and renovating into one category labeled "acting." Assessing and awareness are combined to create the category "starting," while no activity and not applicable are combined to create "no activity." We have done this to simplify the graphs and to more clearly show the patterns of relationships.

Section 4: Conclusions



In contracting for this report, the sponsors desired to present information about the levels of Y2K preparedness that is useful to decision makers in Kansas. Accordingly, our conclusions are concerned mainly with policy implications and steps to take in the near future to continue to address the Y2K problems.



Although our study shows that in all questions and for all types of organizations there are some respondents who feel that their state of preparation is relatively good, there are significant numbers of organizations of all kinds that are only beginning to attack the Y2K problem. This is especially the case for computerized systems and external business support. Identifying these organizations and assisting them is a major public policy issue. A brief article by Jones (1998) notes that organizations which start later to fix the Y2K problem must devote a much higher percentage of information systems staff time to the problem. Organizations starting in, e.g., December of 1998, should expect to allocate roughly 60% of their staff time to the problem and even then a solution is not at all guaranteed.



A second possible policy area arises in the very high portion of respondents of all categories who indicate that they either do not know or have not done anything about their legal relationships with vendors. Not only may this be a signal to policy makers that an educational effort is in order, but also lawmakers might wish to consider possible legislation to control what might become a flood of Y2K-related lawsuits that might appear in the courts not long after the year 2000 begins.



Taken together, there are some concrete steps in the form of educational programs, like the conference sponsored by the Chief Information Technology Officer, which are designed to provide local organizations with information and options for effectively addressing Y2K problems. Similar types of efforts should continue throughout 1999 to spur actions to assess and address possible Y2K problems, and after the year 2000, to learn how local organizations are addressing problems caused by any Y2K failures.



There is also a need for further study to chart the progress of actions on the part of cities, counties, schools, and hospitals to address Y2K problems. We suggest that additional surveys should be taken every six months to document the progress and extent of Y2K activity. After the year 2000, another survey should be taken to assess the impact of Y2K problems, their costs, and the effectiveness of programs to address this issue.
